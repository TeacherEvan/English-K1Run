# English K1 Run Task Notes

## Design Context

_This section synthesizes the current codebase, repository documentation, live UI patterns, and the latest UX defaults gathered on 2026-04-01. When direct stakeholder input is unavailable, follow the strongest product signals below._

### Users

English K1 Run is a teacher-launched, child-played classroom game for kindergarten and early-primary learners. It is used on tablets, touch displays, and shared classroom browsers, often with a teacher starting the activity and children taking short turns or replaying fast rounds. Students need large, obvious interaction targets and instant feedback; teachers need a menu and settings flow that is quick to launch, easy to supervise, and reliable under classroom pressure. The interface should help children practice vocabulary, recognition, and listening in short, repeatable rounds without reading-heavy friction.

### Brand Personality

Warm, encouraging, playful.

The product should feel optimistic and reassuring rather than chaotic or hyper-competitive. It should create delight, confidence, and a sense of “I can do this,” with rewards that feel celebratory but never overwhelming. For teachers, the tone should also feel dependable and calm under repetition. Because the app supports multiple classroom languages, the visual tone should stay universal, friendly, and welcoming across cultures.

### Aesthetic Direction

Lean into a touch-first, nature-backed, toy-like classroom aesthetic with polished game energy. Keep the turtle mascot, large tactile controls, friendly rounded forms, and bright kid-facing colors, but refine them into a more intentional visual system. Favor a light-first presentation for classrooms, with dark mode as optional support rather than the default mood. Use sky and ocean blues, leaf greens, warm sand and trophy golds, and softly tinted neutrals that feel alive instead of generic gray. Nature photography and animal or emoji content are part of the product DNA, so decorative choices should feel outdoorsy, sunny, curious, and alive—not corporate, futuristic, or sterile.

The memorable signature should be a warm nature-meets-playroom world: real outdoor backdrops, a cheerful turtle mascot, and oversized tactile controls that feel made for small hands.

Reference cues already present in the product:

- Touch-first gameplay on large surfaces
- Nature and animal imagery
- Playful motion and celebratory feedback
- Multilingual classroom use
- High readability at distance
- Teacher-friendly launch flow

Anti-direction:

- No generic SaaS dashboard styling
- No dark neon gamer aesthetic
- No glassmorphism as the default styling language
- No overused card-on-card clutter
- No ad-like mobile game chaos
- No tiny controls, thin contrast, or reading-heavy layouts

Color guardrails:

- Prefer sky blue, ocean blue, leaf green, warm sand, and sun-gold accents
- Avoid pure black or pure white, washed-out gray-on-color, and cyan-purple “AI” palettes

### Design Principles

1. **Teacher-launch, child-play.** Setup must be obvious for adults, while interaction stays effortless for young learners.
2. **Distance-readable touch clarity.** Prioritize big targets, instant comprehension, and strong hierarchy over decorative complexity.
3. **Nature-backed delight.** Use the turtle, landscapes, and cheerful color warmth as the product’s memorable signature.
4. **Celebration without chaos.** Motion, color, and reward moments should build confidence without exhausting the classroom.
5. **Accessibility defines the finish.** WCAG AA contrast, reduced-motion respect, multilingual clarity, and color-safe cues are baseline design requirements.
